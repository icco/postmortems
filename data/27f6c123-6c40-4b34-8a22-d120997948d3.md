---

A critical issue affected Linux kernel systems around New Year's 2008-2009, leading to system crashes when a leap second was introduced. The problem was widely reported and reproduced on various distributions, including Fedora 8 with kernel 2.6.26.6.

The root cause was identified as a deadlock within the kernel's timekeeping and logging mechanisms. Specifically, the leap second handling code, invoked from the timer interrupt handler, acquired `xtime_lock`. During this critical section, the code called `printk` to log the leap second event.

The `printk` function, in turn, attempted to wake up `klogd` to process kernel messages. Under conditions where the system was busy, the scheduler, in its attempt to get the current time, would also try to acquire `xtime_lock`. This simultaneous attempt to acquire an already held lock resulted in a deadlock, causing the system to crash.

The immediate impact was system instability and crashes for affected Linux machines. A proposed quick fix involved preventing `printk` calls while `xtime_lock` is held, though a more comprehensive solution that still allowed for leap second notifications was desired.
