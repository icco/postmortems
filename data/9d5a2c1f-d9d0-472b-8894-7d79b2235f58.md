The Mars Pathfinder spacecraft experienced unexpected system resets a few days after its landing on Mars in July 1997. These resets were triggered when a high-priority task, bc_dist, failed to complete its execution before another critical task, bc_sched, began, indicating a missed deadline within the system's real-time operations.

The root cause was identified as a priority inversion problem within the VxWorks operating system. A lower-priority task, ASI/MET, acquired a mutual exclusion semaphore (a shared resource) but was then preempted by several medium-priority tasks. This prevented the ASI/MET task from releasing the semaphore, effectively blocking the higher-priority bc_dist task which needed the same resource.

Each system reset reinitialized hardware and software, terminating current ground-commanded activities. While no already-collected science or engineering data was lost, the remaining activities for that day were delayed until the following day. The problem was exacerbated by higher-than-anticipated data rates and increased science activities on the surface.

The problem was corrected by remotely patching the spacecraft's flight software. The fix involved enabling the priority inheritance option for the mutual exclusion semaphores used by the select() mechanism in the VxWorks operating system. This ensured that when a low-priority task held a resource needed by a high-priority task, its priority would temporarily be elevated to prevent preemption by intermediate-priority tasks.

The issue was diagnosed using built-in debug features, including a trace/log facility, which were designed to remain in the flight software. This allowed engineers to reproduce the issue in a lab setting within 18 hours, making the priority inversion evident. Extensive testing confirmed the fix would not adversely affect other system behaviors or performance.
