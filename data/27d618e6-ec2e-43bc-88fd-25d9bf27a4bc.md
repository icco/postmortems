On July 15, 2015, EVE Online experienced an extended downtime of 699 minutes, one of its longest in years, following a patch deployment. Initial attempts to start the Tranquillity (TQ) cluster after the patch failed, with numerous nodes becoming stuck in the startup sequence, preventing the game from coming online.

The operations team initially suspected environmental or deployment tool issues, attempting multiple restarts and even a rollback test. As these efforts did not resolve the problem, EVE programmers joined the investigation, focusing on code and data aspects. Various theories were explored, including hardware, specific server configurations, and data related to the new sovereignty system.

Through iterative testing, including disabling parts of the sovereignty system and live console experiments, it was discovered that the presence of sovereignty campaign data, specifically its associated logging, was causing nodes to become unresponsive and eventually drop from the cluster. A particular logging channel used by the campaign system on TQ was found to severely degrade node performance.

The immediate resolution involved deploying Hotfix #5, which completely removed all logging within the campaign system. This allowed the TQ cluster to start successfully. After VIP checks and re-enabling CREST, EVE Online was reopened to all players at 22:41 UTC on July 15th.

Further post-incident analysis confirmed that even simple, high-volume logging on the specific campaign channel on TQ could reproduce the node instability, while other logging channels or test servers did not exhibit the same issue. This highlighted a critical performance bottleneck related to the logging infrastructure for that specific feature.
