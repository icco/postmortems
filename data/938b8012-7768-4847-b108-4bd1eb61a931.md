A 2013 commentary on the SEC enforcement order against Knight Capital, summarising the events of August 1, 2012 in which Knight's SMARS order router took out a $465 million trading loss in 45 minutes — effectively bankrupting the firm.

Knight had spent the prior weeks preparing for the launch of NYSE's new Retail Liquidity Program. The required code went into SMARS — Knight's high-speed algorithmic order router — and reused a configuration flag that, in a much older codebase, had activated a separate feature called "Power Peg". Power Peg had been retired in 2003. The dead code remained present and callable in SMARS, and a 2005 refactor moved the cumulative-shares tracking out of Power Peg's hot path without retesting whether Power Peg would still terminate correctly when activated.

The deployment to eight SMARS servers between July 27 and August 1 was performed manually by a single technician with no peer review. Seven servers received the new RLP code; the eighth did not. When the market opened on August 1 and parent orders arrived with the repurposed flag set, the eighth server invoked the still-present Power Peg code path. Without the cumulative-shares tracker that had been moved seven years earlier, Power Peg never satisfied its termination condition and continued issuing child orders without bound.

Beginning at 08:01 ET, an internal Knight system began emitting "BNET reject" emails referencing SMARS and a "Power Peg disabled" error. 97 such messages went out to a Knight distribution list before the 09:30 ET market open. They were not formal alerts and were not reviewed in time.

Knight had no documented incident-response procedure. While SMARS continued to send millions of orphaned child orders into the market, technologists trying to triage the problem uninstalled the new RLP code from the seven correctly-deployed servers — which made things worse, because that activated Power Peg on those servers too. The position took roughly 45 minutes to halt. The SEC ultimately fined Knight $12 million; the firm was acquired shortly after.

The post's lessons-learned framing: deployment scripts that don't verify cluster-wide consistency, no monitoring to catch a release-version mismatch, treating warning emails as ignorable, leaving long-dead code paths reachable, and reusing a flag that previously bound to that dead path. The fix the SEC report recommends is largely procedural; the post argues the procedural fix is downstream of an engineering culture problem.
