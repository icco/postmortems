On July 17, 2020, Cloudflare experienced an outage affecting its backbone network and various services globally. The incident began at 21:12 UTC and the primary service was restored by 21:39 UTC, though full recovery including logs and metrics took until 22:10 UTC. The outage caused traffic to drop by approximately 50% across Cloudflare's network, impacting numerous data centers in North America, Europe, and South America.

The root cause was a configuration error introduced during an attempt to alleviate congestion on the backbone link between Newark and Chicago. A network engineer made an incorrect change on a router in Atlanta. Instead of removing specific Atlanta routes from the backbone, a single line change inadvertently caused all BGP routes to be leaked into the backbone with a higher local-preference.

This misconfiguration instructed the Atlanta router to send all its BGP routes to other backbone routers, and because these routes had a higher local-preference (200) than the local server routes (100), all traffic intended for local compute nodes was redirected to Atlanta. This rapidly overwhelmed the Atlanta router, leading to failures in Cloudflare network locations connected to the backbone.

The outage affected Internet properties and Cloudflare services, causing a significant drop in traffic. Affected locations included major cities across the US (San Jose, Dallas, Seattle, Los Angeles, Chicago, Washington, DC, Richmond, Newark, Atlanta), Europe (London, Amsterdam, Frankfurt, Paris, Stockholm, Moscow, St. Petersburg), and South America (São Paulo, Curitiba, Porto Alegre). Customers in these regions experienced service disruption for 27 minutes.

Cloudflare immediately disabled the overwhelmed Atlanta router to restore service. For long-term remediation, they deployed a change to prevent the recurrence of this specific issue by modifying the BGP local-preference for local server routes, ensuring a single location cannot attract other locations' traffic in a similar manner. They also planned to introduce a maximum-prefix limit on backbone BGP sessions to provide a fail-safe.
