On August 10, 1628, the Swedish warship Vasa embarked on its maiden voyage. Despite being the most powerfully armed warship of its time, it sank within 20 minutes of setting sail, less than a mile into its journey, after being hit by two strong winds and heeling to port.

Recent detailed examinations revealed that the ship was significantly asymmetrical, with more structure on the port side than the starboard. This asymmetry was attributed to the use of different measurement standards (Swedish feet vs. Amsterdam feet) by various workmen during construction, leading to inconsistent dimensions.

Additionally, the Vasa was found to be dangerously top-heavy. The ship's designer, Henrik Hybertsson, who had no prior experience building a ship with two gun decks, overbuilt the deck structure, making it heavier than necessary to carry its 64 bronze cannons. This fundamental design flaw contributed significantly to its instability.

The sinking resulted in the deaths of 30 people. An immediate inquest concluded instability, but the precise reasons remained debated for centuries until modern archaeological research provided definitive answers.

The ship's sinking in Baltic waters helped preserve it for over 300 years. After its recovery, the Vasa now serves as a museum exhibit, and ongoing efforts are focused on its long-term preservation.
