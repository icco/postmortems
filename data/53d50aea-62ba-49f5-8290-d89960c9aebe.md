During the Apollo 11 lunar landing on July 20, 1969, the Lunar Module's (LM) guidance computer experienced multiple "program alarms," specifically 1202 alarms, during the powered descent phase. These alarms signaled that the computer was overloaded and had more tasks than it could process, leading to software restarts.

The root cause of the CPU overload was an uncorrected design flaw in the rendezvous radar interface. The radar was activated prematurely, continuously feeding data to the computer and consuming approximately 13% of its duty cycle, even though its data was not required until later in the descent.

The alarms created a critical situation, requiring rapid assessment by Mission Control. Despite the computer's overload, guidance officers determined that a 1202 alarm was a "go" condition unless it occurred too frequently or the trajectory deviated significantly. This decision allowed the mission to proceed.

The crew was instructed to continue the landing, and the mission successfully concluded with the lunar touchdown. The operational decision to proceed, based on prior analysis of the alarm's severity, served as the immediate resolution for the ongoing computer overload during the critical descent phase.
