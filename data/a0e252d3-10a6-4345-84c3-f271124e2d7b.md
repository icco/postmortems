On July 2, 2019, Cloudflare experienced a global outage lasting 27 minutes, from 13:42 UTC to 14:09 UTC. The incident caused Cloudflare customers and their users to see 502 error pages, as the company's core proxying, CDN, and WAF functionality became unavailable due to CPU exhaustion across its worldwide network.

The root cause was the deployment of a new rule within Cloudflare's WAF Managed Rules. This rule contained a poorly written regular expression that led to excessive backtracking, consuming nearly 100% of CPU resources on servers handling HTTP/HTTPS traffic. The rapid global deployment mechanism, Quicksilver, distributed the problematic rule across the entire network in seconds.

Several factors contributed to the widespread impact. A protection mechanism designed to prevent excessive CPU usage by regular expressions had been mistakenly removed during a prior WAF refactoring. The WAF's test suite also lacked the capability to identify such CPU consumption issues. Furthermore, the standard operating procedure for WAF rule changes allowed for immediate global deployment without staged rollouts, unlike other software releases.

Cloudflare's internal systems, including the control panel and Jira, were also affected, hindering the incident response due to reliance on the very services that were down. SREs faced challenges accessing systems due to security-related credential timeouts, and the status page was not updated quickly enough.

In response, Cloudflare immediately re-introduced the missing CPU usage protection, manually inspected all existing WAF rules, and committed to implementing performance profiling in their test suite. They are also switching to regex engines with runtime guarantees and revising WAF deployment SOPs to include staged rollouts. Long-term plans include porting the WAF to a new firewall engine for enhanced performance and protection.
